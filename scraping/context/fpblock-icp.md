What Defines Our ICP
We work with teams that need to move fast, but cannot afford to be wrong.
Our ICP is defined by decision pressure under permanence, not by industry, funding stage, or technology choice.

The Core ICP Test
A team is a real ICP when 4 of 5 are true:
Being wrong carries a clear financial, reputational, or legal cost
That cost is persistent or externally visible
They are already aware of this risk
Decisions accelerate once clarity appears
They value avoiding irreversible regret more than maximising short‑term speed
If fewer than 4 are true → do not advance.

One‑Sentence ICP Profiles (2026)
Regret‑Aware Builders — Teams making irreversible system decisions where failure would create lasting financial, reputational, or legal damage.
Systemically Exposed Operators — Teams running live, persistent systems where failures propagate directly to users and cannot be quietly reset.
Regulated or Regulation‑Bound Teams — Teams whose systems must withstand audits, enforcement, or legal challenge once deployed.
Infrastructure Stewards — Teams responsible for long‑lived systems where ownership, explainability, and failure accountability matter more than feature velocity.
AI‑Accelerated, Decision‑Heavy Teams — Teams shipping quickly with AI where execution is cheap but architectural mistakes permanently lock in risk.

Cost of Being Wrong (At Least One Must Be Present)
Financial
Real money, assets, or revenue at risk
Expensive or impossible redesigns
Reputational
Public failure or trust erosion
Long or uncertain recovery timelines
Legal / Regulatory
Compliance scrutiny or audits
Personal or corporate liability
If none are clearly present → not our ICP.

One‑Sentence Firm Disqualifiers
MVP‑to‑Get‑Funded Teams — Building primarily to raise capital rather than live with consequences.
Uniform‑Speed Thinkers — Treating all decisions as equally reversible.
“We’ll Fix It Later” Teams — Assuming architectural regret can always be refactored away.
No Downside Owner — No individual clearly accountable for failure.
Internal‑Only Consequences — Being wrong only causes internal rework.
Evangelism‑Required Prospects — Needing persuasion that correctness or trust matter.
Tool‑First Buyers — Optimising stacks instead of incentives, ownership, and failure modes.
Judgment‑Averse Buyers — Willing to pay for delivery but not thinking.
If any one is dominant → pause.

Speed Doctrine (Important)
We value speed where failure is cheap and correctness where failure is expensive.
Fail fast on reversible decisions.
Slow down only where mistakes persist.

FP Block — Disqualifier Maturation & Holding Pattern
Purpose
This document defines how FP Block allows disqualified teams to mature naturally without nurturing, chasing, or lowering standards.
The objective is simple:
Remain visible and legible until decision pressure becomes unavoidable.
This is not a funnel, pipeline, or nurture system.
It is a holding pattern grounded in reality.

Core Principle
We do not convince teams to care — we stay visible until caring becomes unavoidable.
Disqualifiers are not rejected forever.
They are simply not yet under irreversible pressure.

1‑Sentence Narratives That Mature Disqualifiers
These narratives are pressure mirrors.
They surface reality without instruction, persuasion, or calls to action.
Speed feels free until you discover which decisions couldn’t be undone.
Most teams don’t fail because they moved too slowly, but because they moved fast in places that later became permanent.
Iteration only works when the cost of being wrong stays low.
The hardest problems don’t show up at launch — they show up once change becomes expensive.
AI makes it easier to ship, but harder to see which assumptions you’re locking in.
Regret usually arrives long after the decision that caused it.
If failure is public, correctness stops being optional.
Some mistakes teach you quickly; others teach you forever.
Most systems work until the moment they need to be trusted.
Being early only helps if you can live with what you made permanent.
These sentences should feel uncomfortable, not motivating.

Holding Pattern (Non‑Sales)
This is how FP Block remains present without nurturing.
We share how teams get into trouble — and how some avoid it — without naming solutions.
We host conversations about decisions people wish they’d delayed.
We write about where failure becomes expensive, not where success looks impressive.
We explore why certain choices age badly, even when they seemed reasonable at the time.
We make irreversible tradeoffs visible before they show up as problems.
We stay close to the moments where systems stop being flexible.
We don’t follow up — we stay legible.
We let timing declare itself.

Usage Rules
Do not send these narratives directly to disqualified teams
Do not follow up after disqualification
Do not track or score engagement
Do not add calls to action
These narratives exist to be encountered, not consumed.

Final Guardrail
If a team becomes qualified, it should feel obvious on both sides.
If you have to re‑engage them, timing has not arrived.
