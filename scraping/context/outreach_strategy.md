# FP Block — EthCC Cannes 2026 Outreach Strategy

---

## 1. Executive Summary

FP Block attends EthCC Cannes in June 2026 with a single, focused objective: find the teams and individuals who are already living with the weight of irreversible decisions.

Not teams who are thinking about it. Not teams who might feel it someday. Teams who feel it now — who have locked capital, regulated obligations, or permanently visible systems where being wrong carries a cost they cannot erase.

EthCC Cannes is a concentration of exactly those people. The speaker list alone contains custody providers managing institutional funds, regulated banks issuing tokenized securities, security auditors whose misses cause permanent exploits, ZK infrastructure teams whose proof errors propagate silently downstream, and law firms whose advice is etched into compliance records. These are not exploratory conversations. These are conversations with operators who already understand that some decisions cannot be walked back.

**What success looks like at EthCC Cannes:**

- 8–12 high-quality conversations with Tier 1 or Tier 2 ICP-matched individuals
- At least 3 post-conference engagements that advance to a scoping or architecture discussion within 60 days
- FP Block's name planted in the minds of 20+ Tier 1 and Tier 2 operators as the firm that thinks seriously about permanence, isolation, and trust boundaries
- Zero time spent on disqualified prospects

Success is not measured in business cards collected or LinkedIn connections made. It is measured in conversations where the other person names a problem before FP Block does.

## 8. At-Conference Playbook

### General Posture

FP Block is not at EthCC to collect meetings. FP Block is at EthCC to find the conversations that are already trying to happen — where someone is already feeling the weight of a decision they can't walk back, and hasn't yet found the right framing or partner.

The job at the conference is to listen more than speak. When someone describes their infrastructure situation, the question is: does this person have a problem that FP Block can actually solve, or are they building for a world where the stakes are still theoretical?

### Spotting Qualifying Conversations

**Strong ICP signals in unscripted conversation:**

- They use words like "we can't afford to get this wrong" or "once this is live, it's live"
- They reference regulatory scrutiny, auditors, or legal counsel as part of their infrastructure decisions
- They describe failure modes in terms of user impact, not internal rework
- They talk about the permanence of what they're building — titling, custody, settlement, issuance
- They have specific, named concerns about isolation, blast radius, or predictable behavior under load
- They have been wrong before (or know someone who has) and it left a mark

**Weak ICP signals — do not pursue further:**

- They talk about "moving fast" as a primary value without caveats
- Their failure scenario is "we'd have to refactor" or "we'd lose some community trust"
- They are primarily concerned with TVL, DAU, or token price metrics
- They describe their architecture as "we'll upgrade the contract if something breaks"
- They have no individual who is clearly accountable if things go wrong

### Transitioning from Casual to Meeting Request

Do not pitch in hallways. The goal of a hallway conversation is to establish that there is a real conversation worth having — not to have it there.

**Transition language:**

"This is exactly the kind of thing I'd want to go deeper on. Are you around tomorrow afternoon? 20 minutes would be enough to figure out if there's something worth exploring."

"What you're describing is a specific problem we've thought a lot about. I don't want to compress it into a hallway conversation. Could we find 20 minutes somewhere quieter?"

Never ask for "a call" or "a meeting." Ask for time to continue a specific conversation that has already started.

### Meeting Length and Structure

20 minutes. No deck. No demo.

The structure of a good meeting:
1. (5 minutes) Let them describe their current architecture and decision context in their own words
2. (5 minutes) Ask one or two questions that reveal whether permanence and trust boundaries are live concerns for them
3. (5 minutes) If the ICP signals are strong, name the problem you've heard them describe and offer the Kolme framing
4. (5 minutes) Determine whether there is a specific decision they are currently facing where FP Block could be useful, and agree on a next step

If by minute 10 there are no ICP signals, close gracefully: "I appreciate the context. This may not be the right fit right now — but I'm glad we talked." Then move on.

### Session Attendance

Attend sessions given by Tier 1 and Tier 2 speakers not yet engaged. Listen for their specific framing of problems. Reference it afterward. "I heard you mention [X] in your talk — that's exactly the tension we've been thinking about."

## 9. Message Framework

### Core Principle

The ICP is already aware of their problem. FP Block does not need to convince them that correctness matters, that permanence is real, or that some decisions cannot be undone. They know. The message is: "We build for teams in exactly your position."

### What to Lead With

- Their operational context: what they are running, what is at stake, what they cannot afford to get wrong
- The specific nature of permanence in their domain (custodied assets, regulatory records, on-chain settlement, issued tokens)
- A question, not a statement: "How are you thinking about [isolation / trust boundaries / audit trail] for [their specific system]?"

### What to Avoid Leading With

- The name "Kolme" or "application-specific blockchain" before context is established
- Web3 labels, ecosystem jargon, protocol names
- Superlatives: "fastest," "most interoperable," "best-in-class"
- Anything that sounds like a feature list
- The phrase "blockchain infrastructure" before they have named their problem

### The One-Paragraph Position Statement (Spoken)

"FP Block builds application-specific blockchain infrastructure for teams that are already running systems where being wrong is expensive and visible. We built a framework called Kolme that gives teams the isolation and performance of a dedicated chain without sacrificing interoperability with Ethereum, Solana, or Cosmos. The teams we work with are the ones who've already decided they can't rely on infrastructure they don't control — because the cost of finding out they were wrong is too high."

### Opening Message Structure (Written)

**For LinkedIn:**

> [Their name], I'll be at EthCC Cannes in June and noticed you're speaking on [topic]. [One sentence on what they're building and what's at stake.] I'm curious how your team is thinking about [specific relevant tension — isolation, audit trail, trust boundaries, blast radius]. FP Block works with teams in [their position]. Happy to find 20 minutes at the conference if it's useful.

**For Twitter DM:**

> Hey [name] — saw your [recent post / talk / thread] on [specific topic]. The [specific point they made] is exactly the kind of infrastructure tension we work on at FP Block. Going to be at EthCC Cannes — would be good to talk if you have 20 minutes.

### Personalization by Segment

- **Regulated Custodians:** Reference their regulator, their AUC, and the specificity of "being wrong" in their context. Do not use generic custody language — name the actual cost.
- **Security/Audit Firms:** Reference the systems they protect. The conversation is peer-to-peer, not vendor-to-buyer. They think about failure modes all day; FP Block does too.
- **RWA Tokenization:** Reference the legal permanence of issued tokens. Once a T-bill or invoice is on-chain, the infrastructure choice is embedded in every transaction record.
- **DeFi with Locked TVL:** Reference the blast radius of shared infrastructure failures. The question is isolation, not capability.
- **ZK Infrastructure:** Reference proof correctness and the silent propagation of errors downstream. Technical peer conversation.
- **Law Firms / Compliance:** Reference audit trails, explainability, and what their clients need to present to regulators.

## 12. Meeting Quality Over Quantity

### The Wrong Metric

Number of meetings scheduled. Number of business cards collected. Number of LinkedIn connections made at the event.

### The Right Metric

Number of conversations where the other person named a problem that FP Block can actually solve, before FP Block described the solution.

### Strong ICP Signals During In-Person Conversations

These are the signals that a conversation is worth advancing:

1. **They describe a specific, named risk** — not "we think about security" but "we had an incident last year that we couldn't roll back, and we've been rethinking our architecture since"

2. **They reference an external accountability structure** — a regulator, a board, a legal counsel, an institutional client whose expectations they cannot disappoint

3. **They use permanence language unprompted** — "once we go live," "we can't change this after," "our clients are depending on this being right from day one"

4. **They distinguish between fast and correct without being asked** — "we move quickly on things that are reversible, but this is not one of those"

5. **They have a named decision in front of them** — not theoretical interest in blockchain infrastructure, but a specific architectural choice they are currently facing

6. **The conversation accelerates** — when FP Block names a problem or frames an angle, they lean in rather than politely engaging. Urgency signals readiness.

### Weak Signals — Do Not Advance

- "Interesting, I'll have someone on my team look into it"
- Asking for a deck or a brochure without asking a follow-up question
- Enthusiasm about "the space" rather than their specific problem
- No individual who is clearly accountable if the conversation goes nowhere
- The problem they describe could be solved by refactoring in six months

### Quality Standard: The Regret Test

Would this person, if their infrastructure failed in the way they've described, experience regret that lasts longer than a quarter? If yes, they are ICP. If their failure scenario ends with "we'd have to refactor" or "our token price would dip," they are not.

## 13. Follow-Up Protocol

### Principle

FP Block does not chase. The ICP is already aware of their problem. When timing is right, decisions accelerate. The job after EthCC is to stay present without creating noise.

### Within 48 Hours of Conference End

Send a brief, specific note to every conversation that showed two or more strong ICP signals. The note should:

- Reference the specific thing they said (not "great to meet you at EthCC")
- Name the specific problem or tension you discussed
- Offer one concrete next step: a technical call, a review of a specific architecture question, a document they might find useful
- Not include a deck, a pitch, or any request that requires them to do work

**Template:**

> [Name] — good to talk at EthCC. When you mentioned [specific thing they said about their infrastructure / the decision they're facing], that's exactly the problem we've been working on. If you want to go deeper on [specific aspect], I'm happy to set aside time. No agenda beyond that.

### Weeks 2–4 Post-Conference

If no response: one follow-up, maximum. Reference something they have done or said publicly since the conference (a post, a talk, a product announcement). Do not reference the original message.

If they respond but are not ready: acknowledge the timing, say you'll check back in [specific timeframe they suggested or implied], and honor that. Do not follow up before that date.

If they are ready: move immediately. Do not let a ready ICP wait while FP Block schedules internal review.

### After 30 Days with No Engagement

Move them to passive monitoring. No further outreach. If they post something publicly that directly connects to the conversation, a single comment or reply is appropriate. If they reach out, respond immediately.

### The Timing Doctrine

Clarity creates motion. The right prospects move when they are ready, not when FP Block is persistent. A non-response is not a rejection — it is a timing signal. The goal after EthCC is to be the team they think of when their timing finally arrives.

Do not mistake silence for disqualification. Do mistake chasing for qualification.
